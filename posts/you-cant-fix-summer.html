You Can't Fix Summer

Sarah Showers · July 6, 2026 · 5 min

A follow-up to the luxury tax post. There's a part of summer no printable will fix. There's also a part you're carrying that you don't have to carry the same way.

A couple of weeks ago I wrote that summer break is a luxury tax on parents, and that the bill comes due differently depending on what you can afford. I stand by all of it. But the part that stuck with people most, going by the messages I got, wasn't the camp math. It was the packing. The bathing suit, the labeled sunscreen, the water bottle they will absolutely lose, every single morning, for ten weeks, before you have to be on a call.

That line landed because it's the part almost everyone recognizes. It's also the part I want to come back to, because it's the one piece of this whole situation you actually have some control over.

Let me be careful here, though, because I don't want to do the thing I criticized last time.

First, the part no system fixes

If you can't afford camp, a weekly planner is not your problem and it is not your solution. If summer means losing the free lunch your kids rely on during the school year, or cobbling together coverage from a village that's already stretched thin, that's a structural failure, and I'm not going to hand you a printable and call it help. Naming that honestly was the whole point of the last post.

So this isn't a post about fixing summer. Summer, as currently built, cannot be fixed by any one family.

This is a post about the narrower thing. The daily logistics. The stuff that lives in your head and wakes you up at 5am running the day before it's even started. That part is real work, it falls mostly on one person, and it responds to being handled differently in a way the big structural stuff does not.

The problem isn't that it's invisible. It's that it's undocumented.

Here's the reframe that changed how I think about my own summers.

The mental load of summer feels invisible, and we tend to talk about it that way. But invisible isn't quite the right word. The work isn't hidden. It's just living in the worst possible place: your head. Every camp schedule, every gap week, every "wait, is it pajama day or water day," every kid's specific lunch preferences, all of it stored in one already-tired brain with no backup.

When something lives only in your head, three things happen. You have to re-decide it constantly, because nothing is ever settled. You can't hand any of it off, because handing off requires explaining, and explaining is often more work than just doing it yourself. And you carry a low background hum of anxiety all day, because some part of you knows that if you forget, no one else will catch it.

The fix for that isn't to try harder. It's to get it out of your head and onto something you can look at. Something the whole household can look at.

What "getting it out of your head" actually looks like

This is the method I put in this week's newsletter, and it's almost embarrassingly simple. I call it the Little Board.

One small surface, whatever you already have. A cheap whiteboard, a printed sheet on the fridge, a laminated card in a drawer. On it, one week at a time, you write the handful of things that otherwise get re-decided every morning:

  • What each kid needs each day (water day, field trip, show-and-tell, or nothing)
  • What's in the lunch rotation this week, decided once on Sunday, not relitigated at 7:15
  • Who's doing drop-off and who's doing pickup
  • The one gap or oddity this week that will bite you if you forget it

That's the whole thing. It isn't an app. It doesn't sync. It doesn't need a subscription or a login or a family meeting. It just moves the week out of one person's head and onto a surface everyone can see.

The reason it works isn't the format. It's what the format does to the load. Once the week is written down, you stop being the only copy. Your partner can read the board instead of asking you. Your kid can check the board instead of asking you. And you can look at it on Sunday, make five small decisions at once, and then not carry them for the rest of the week.

Why decide once instead of every day

The exhausting thing about summer isn't any single decision. It's that you make the same decisions over and over, because none of them ever get to stay decided.

Deciding lunch on Sunday for the whole week is not more work than deciding it daily. It's dramatically less. You're batching one annoying task instead of spreading it across five stressful mornings. The grocery list nearly writes itself, because you already know what the week needs. And on the mornings when everything's going sideways, you're not also trying to invent lunch from scratch. You're just reading a board.

None of this makes summer easy. The camp costs are still absurd. The schedule is still a patchwork. The structural stuff I wrote about last time is still true, and still needs to be said louder than a blog post can say it.

But the part that's yours to carry, you don't have to carry the same way. You can set it down somewhere you can see it, and let the rest of the household see it too. That alone is worth more than it sounds like at 7:15 in the morning.
