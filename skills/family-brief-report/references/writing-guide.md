# Family Brief Writing Guide

## Voice

Warm, precise, unhurried. Write like a thoughtful friend who happens to be very organized — not a consultant. Second person ("you", "your mornings"), never "the client." Plain words over jargon: "the running list in your head," not "cognitive load distribution." Quote the client's own phrases back to them in italics where they were vivid — hearing your own words reflected is the strongest "she got it" signal in the report.

Reframe relentlessly: the problem is always the *system* (no shared calendar, no night-before ritual, invisible work landing on one person), never the *person*. If the client said something self-critical, the report may gently contradict it with evidence from their own session.

Use real names everywhere the client used them: the spouse, each kid, even the grandparents' nicknames. "Ada's craft ideas" and "firmly Dalton's department" do more for the heard-feeling than any amount of polish. Never use a name the client didn't say.

## Sounding human (read this twice)

A client who senses machine writing loses the entire "she got it" effect in one sentence, so this section outranks everything else stylistically. The habits below are recognizable AI fingerprints. Avoid them:

- **Em dashes.** One or two in the whole report, maximum. Everywhere you reach for one, use a comma, a period, parentheses, or the word "and" instead. If a sentence falls apart without its dash, rewrite the sentence.
- **The reversal snap.** "It isn't effort you're missing. It's a system." / "Not meal planning — a menu." This X-versus-Y pivot is the single most recognizable tell. Say the plain version: "you're missing a system, not effort."
- **Mirrored fragments.** "Your words. Our blueprint." Clipped two-beat endings read as copywriting, not care. Let the sentence breathe: "You said it yourself, and this plan is built on it."
- **Punchline colons and constant triads.** A colon that sets up a zinger, or a rhythmic list of three in every other sentence, both register as performance. One triad per page is plenty.
- **Compressed cleverness.** "Night-before prep, made ten minutes small." If a phrase feels quotable, ask whether a warm, organized friend writing a note would actually put it that way. Usually she'd say "ten minutes the night before saves the whole morning."

Warmth lives in slightly loose, ordinary sentences with "and" and "so" doing the connecting, contractions throughout, and the occasional aside in parentheses. Read every page aloud (literally); anything you wouldn't say across a kitchen table gets rewritten until you would.

## The two jobs of the report

The deck balances two kinds of value, and both must be present at full strength:

1. **The mirror** (pages 02, 03, 04, 06): the client sees her own life accurately described, in her own words, with the invisible work made visible. These pages are prose-dense on purpose. Don't compress them into bullet fragments; that's where the "she got it" feeling lives.
2. **The takeaway** (pages 05, 07–13): concrete, small, sequenced actions with honest costs and honest payoffs. One more rule here: aspirations stay aspirations. If the client *wants* the gym streak back, write "the room you'd finally have," not phrasing that implies she already has it. Misstating where they are, even optimistically, breaks the heard-feeling as fast as an AI tell.

A report that's all mirror feels lovely but goes nowhere; all takeaway feels like a listicle from someone who wasn't listening.

## Page-by-page

Length budgets matter — pages are fixed-size. Stay within them.

### 01 — Cover
- "Prepared with care for" → client's full name (e.g., "Laura Langston").
- Session Date → e.g., "June 11, 2026". Your Family → short descriptor (e.g., "Two girls & two dogs"; ≤6 words). Primary Focus → the dominant theme in ≤5 words, ideally the client's own phrase (e.g., "The bookends of the day").

### 02 — Your Family, At a Glance (prose page)
Four paragraphs plus a pull quote. Three paragraphs is too thin — clients respond strongly to seeing their household reflected in real detail, and this page is the first signal that you were listening. Aim for roughly 80/70/60/50 words across the four paragraphs:

1. **Who's in the house**: everyone by name — kids (names and ages), spouse, pets, grandparents and their role. Specific, not generic. "Ada is six, creative, and full of plans" does more than "they have two daughters."
2. **How the days actually run**: work schedules, who covers drop-off/pickup, any schedule quirks or seasonal shifts (camp vs. school year), and what the rhythms depend on going right.
3. **A human moment from the session**: something that happened the week you spoke, something they're proud of, or something that shows how the household keeps moving through hard days. This is the paragraph clients read twice.
4. **Anything about them that shapes the whole brief**: neurodivergence, morning/evening tendencies, past system failures, a core value they named. State it plainly and without drama — it sets up every recommendation that follows.

Pull quote: a verbatim line from the client that captures how the household *feels* from inside — not a summary of what they said, their actual words.

### 03 — What We Heard (executive summary)
- **The Friction** (2 short paragraphs, ~30 words each): the core pain, concrete and specific, using their examples.
- **The Family You're Building** (2 short paragraphs, ~25 words each): their stated values and aspirations — what they're *for*, not what's broken.
- **Hero stat** (rose card): hours back per week, as a number like "5+". See Estimates below.
- **Our Focus**: one italic phrase (≤8 words) naming the strategy of the whole brief.

### 04 — The Invisible Load (prose page)
The single most heard-making page: every untracked thing the client carries, written down in one place. Eight items across two columns, each ~12 words, all verbatim-grounded (if she mentioned the pen pal letter, the pen pal letter is here). Bottom row: rose card with the client's most self-questioning quote, white card beside it with an honest, warm answer (~55 words) that reframes the chaos as a capacity problem, not a character problem.

### 05 — First Things First (effort/relief spectrum)
Three zones, left to right, on a gradient track from rose (most relief, least effort) to muted taupe (least relief, most effort). Each zone has an eyebrow label, a serif title, a short description, and a list of items. Fill all three zones from the session:

- **Zone 1 — Quick Wins** (eyebrow: "Start here · this week"): List all six quick wins by short name (e.g., "Google Calendar brain dump", "Trash and recycling, to Dalton"). These are the same six from pages 07–08 — the names here are just the anchor, detail comes later.
- **Zone 2 — Worth the Work** (eyebrow: "Once quick wins feel settled"): 2–3 bigger lifts from page 11. Short phrases, same items.
- **Zone 3 — Not Right Now** (eyebrow: "Someday, or never"): 1–2 things the client mentioned that aren't worth the effort, or that they were hesitant about. Give them explicit permission to skip these.

The description line for each zone is pre-written in the template — leave it. Only fill the item lists.

### 06 — What You've Already Tried (prose page)
Six rows: tool/system name plus what happened (~20 words each), told without judgment. Include what *worked* as well as what didn't. End with the rose lesson bar (~35 words): the pattern across their history that every recommendation in the brief obeys. This page is what makes the recommendations feel derived from their life rather than generic; if the session revealed no clear pattern, find the honest one that's there (it always exists).

### 07 — Your Quick Wins (6 rows)
Each row: **pain** (≤12 words, in their language) → **fix** (≤14 words, concrete and doable this week). Big-relief/small-lift only. Quick wins are behaviors and ownership shifts, not purchases. Prefer whole-job handoffs over shared reminders when the spouse has a track record with clear-edged jobs (his, like the dogs, beats two more phone alerts).

### 08 — What We Recommend (6 cards)
Each card: **tool** name, **use** (~22 words: what it does for *this* family, tied to a session detail), **Cost** (e.g., "Free", "$8/mo", "Already paying"), **Setup** (e.g., "20 min"), **Gives back** (e.g., "~1 hr/wk").
Order: lead with upgrades to systems they already touch; introduce at most 2–3 genuinely new things. "Tools" may include rituals and conversations as well as apps/products.

### 09 — How Each One Works (6 rows)
The friction-remover, especially for neurodivergent clients: every recommendation gets exact steps and an exact "when," so nothing requires planning energy to start. Each row: **tool** name, **steps** (~25 words: concrete actions a tired person can follow verbatim, including where things go and what "done" looks like), **when** chip (e.g., "Day 1", "Sunday", "Week 2"). The whens must agree with pages 10 and 11; one timeline across the whole brief, never two.

### 10 — Your First Week (4 days)
A gentle on-ramp: Day 1–4, each with a **task** (~20 words, single concrete action ≤15 minutes) and the **tool** it uses (from page 08). Sequence so each day builds on the last. Day 1 should be almost embarrassingly easy.

### 11 — What Comes Next (3 items)
The "Worth the Work" tier: bigger lifts deferred until quick wins are settled. Each: **txt** (~12 words, serif headline style) + **when** (e.g., "Weeks 3–4", "When school starts"). Respect stated hesitations: if they're unsure about a tool, the "when" can be "only if wanted."

### 12 — What You Get Back (payoff)
- **Time Returned** (rose hero): same number as page 03's hero stat; **cap** line (~15 words) grounding it.
- **Cost of New Tools**: one honest number with a label that matches it (if the only spend is one-time, the big number is the one-time total labeled "Total Cost," never "$0/month" with a different figure underneath; the headline and the fine print must agree).
- **Worth It Because** (2 lines, ~20 words each): tie back to *their* stated wish using their words, and quote their own insight back if they gave you one.

### 13 — Your Next Steps (2 steps)
Open with a one-line lead under the title (~30 words) that says the kind thing they need to hear, by name (e.g., "You're already doing the hard part, Laura"). Then:

- **Step 1 sh**: review-and-react ask naming the spouse; **sd** (~25 words) on why their honest reaction shapes everything and there are no wrong answers.
- **Step 2 sh**: warm, open-ended invitation — not a booking pitch. Something like "My door is always open." **sd** (~30 words): questions, updates, something that didn't work — reach out anytime. This brief is a starting point, not a verdict.
- **CTA**: plain stacked text — "Reach me at" label (small caps, muted) above the email address (serif, rose). The email is `sarah@yourfamilybrief.com`. Do not use a button or a hyperlink — both render poorly in PDF.

## Estimates (hours back, costs)

Build the hours-back number bottom-up from the session, and keep an internal tally you can show Sarah:
- Only count time the client actually described losing (late scrambles, improvised dinners, chase-up texts, bedtime drag).
- Credit each recommendation conservatively (15–60 min/week each); sum, round *down* to a friendly number, present with "+" (e.g., "5+").
- Costs: list real current prices for tools; if the client already pays for it (e.g., an existing subscription), count it as $0 new spend and say so — "already paying" is a feature.

## Prior sessions

If the client has talked with Sarah before, ask Sarah for (or fetch) the earlier transcript too. Details from previous conversations (an appointment that got dropped by a provider, a tool that backfired, a phrase the client used once) woven into pages 02, 04, and 06 are disproportionately powerful: they prove the listening spans the whole relationship, not just one call.
